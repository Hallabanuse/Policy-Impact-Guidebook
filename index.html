PFAS CASE STUDY

Policy Context Assessment Tool

This tool helps teams assess the political context surrounding a specific issue. Based on John W. Kingdon's Multiple Streams Framework, it supports a systematic analysis of three streams: Problems, Politics, and Policies, as well as their convergence (Coupling). The goal is to identify critical moments for policy change - policy windows - and determine strategic entry points for engagement.

Problems
In the Problems Stream, actors in and around government recognise certain conditions as problems. Problem recognition is influenced by crises and disasters, strong symbols, personal experiences of policy actors, and feedback on current programmes.
Key questions: Was there a crisis or scheduled feedback on the functioning of the current legal framework? Are there any crises/disasters, studies, or developments that change the attention for the problem? Are there actors or decision-makers with experiences tied to the issue?

Streams flow independently but interact
Politics
In the Politics Stream, different processes influence what the political agenda looks like. It has three major components: publics, interest groups, and government. Key actors participate in the politics stream and their level of influence on agenda-setting depends on the topic and presence of other forces.
Key questions: Who are the key publics? What is their current opinion on the topic/issue? Who are the key actors & interest groups that have a strong influence? Who are key decision-makers that will likely have a strong influence on the political agenda? What key political events are coming up that will affect the issue?

Policies
The Policies Stream consists of ideas, speeches, proposals, instruments, and solutions that are floating around and could potentially become relevant policy solutions. Solutions are assessed on technical feasibility, value compatibility, budget constraints, public acceptability, and political receptivity.
Key questions: Are there feasible policy solutions available? Do we need more? Do we need to improve the existing ones? What is the level of agreement in the policy/tech community? How does the general public feel about particular solutions? Do we foresee any constraints to the acceptability of solutions?

Coupling
When the three streams converge - a problem is recognised, political receptivity exists, and a viable solution is available - a policy window opens. Windows are scarce and often short.
The Recognised Problem
What is the description of the problem that is recognised by decision-makers?
Human health: contaminated food and drinking water affecting nearly all citizens
Unsustainable costs: €18 billion annually for water treatment - public bears costs
Economic risk: Tourism sector and agricultural production threatened
Political Receptivity
Which key political actor is open to what issues?
Parliamentarians with strong links to communities affected by health risks
Expected new government: "Water as most precious resource" as key priority
Economic framing: "Strengthening the economy" aligns with cost-burden arguments
Feasible Policy Solution
Which solution meets these criteria: technically feasible, value compatible, budget acceptable, politically receptive?
Regulation of non-essential applications of PFAS (distinguishing essential from non-essential uses)
Supported by: EU drinking water limits (2026), ongoing specialist discussions
POLICY WINDOW STATUS
OPENING
Type: Political window (elections)
Predictability: High

Strategic Implications

Action required: Prepare proposals before window opens fully

Key strategies:

  • Counter "blanket ban" framing with "non-essential use" messaging
  • Form alliances with essential applications industries
  • Highlight community health impacts to receptive parliamentarians
  • Emphasise economic arguments (treatment costs)

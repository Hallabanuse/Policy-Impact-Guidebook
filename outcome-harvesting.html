Outcome Harvesting for Policy Engagement

Capturing and documenting observed changes in the behaviour, relationships, actions, or policies of social actors that your project has influenced. This approach is particularly suited for complex policy contexts where change pathways are non-linear and unpredictable.

Why Outcome Harvesting for Policy Work?

Traditional monitoring approaches often struggle in policy and advocacy contexts. Pre-defined indicators assume we know what success will look like and when it will happen. But policy change is rarely linear: unexpected windows of opportunity open, strategies need to pivot, and influence often manifests in ways we didn't anticipate.

The Challenge of Attributing Influence

Policy outcomes result from multiple actors and forces converging. A regulation passes not because of one organisation's advocacy alone, but because of the combined effect of public pressure, political timing, scientific evidence, media coverage, and often events beyond anyone's control. Outcome Harvesting doesn't claim to prove causation - instead, it documents plausible contribution through evidence-based narratives.

What Makes Outcome Harvesting Different

Traditional M&E: Define indicators upfront, then measure whether targets were hit.

Outcome Harvesting: Work backwards from observed changes to understand what happened and how your work contributed.

This "harvesting" approach is particularly valuable when:

  • Outcomes emerge unpredictably from complex systems
  • Your influence is indirect (through coalitions, media, etc.)
  • The policy timeline extends beyond your project cycle
  • You need to capture "negative" outcomes (e.g., successfully blocking harmful policies)

The Six Elements of an Outcome Statement

Every harvested outcome is documented through a structured statement that answers:

Element Question Example
Who Which social actor changed? The Environment Minister
What What did they do differently? Publicly endorsed strict regulation
When/Where When and in what context? At the parliamentary committee hearing, March 2026
Significance Why does this matter? This signals the government's likely vote position
Contribution How did our work contribute? Our briefing on treatment costs was cited in her statement
Evidence How do we know this happened? Official transcript, media coverage, meeting notes

AI-Assisted Outcome Discovery

One of the practical challenges of Outcome Harvesting is the labour-intensive process of finding outcomes. Staff must constantly monitor news, policy documents, social media, and other sources to spot when relevant actors change their behaviour or positions. Emerging AI tools can significantly reduce this burden through automated monitoring and intelligent filtering.

How AI Can Support Outcome Harvesting

  • Automated media monitoring: AI can continuously scan news sources, press releases, and parliamentary records for mentions of your target actors and issues, flagging potential outcomes for human review.
  • Social media analysis: Track when politicians, officials, or organisations change their public positions on social platforms - often an early signal of shifting stances.
  • Document analysis: AI can process large volumes of policy documents, consultation responses, and official communications to identify changes in language, positions, or commitments over time.
  • Pattern recognition: Identify when multiple actors begin using similar framings or arguments - potentially evidence that your communication strategy is gaining traction.
  • Alert systems: Set up automated notifications when key decision points approach (votes, consultations, hearings) to ensure systematic harvesting around critical moments.

Important Considerations

  • Human judgment remains essential: AI can find potential outcomes, but assessing significance and contribution requires human understanding of context.
  • Verification is critical: AI-flagged outcomes must be verified through primary sources before being formally documented.
  • Privacy and ethics: Ensure monitoring practices comply with data protection regulations and ethical standards.
  • Don't over-claim contribution: Just because your framing appears in public discourse doesn't mean you caused it - be rigorous about contribution evidence.

Exemplary Outcome Statements

The following examples illustrate how outcomes might be documented for the PFAS policy engagement case. Both relate to the same intermediate result in our Theory of Change: achieving government support for strong regulation with limited exemptions.

Theory of Change Result

Government officials support strong PFAS regulation (limited exemptions) in the regional vote

These harvested outcomes represent observable changes in behaviour that provide evidence of progress toward this result. Each captures a specific moment when a social actor did something different that matters for our strategy.

Outcome 1: Ministry Adopts Economic Framing

Harvested: November 2025
Who Changed
The Deputy Director of the Environment Ministry's Chemicals Division
What Changed
In an inter-ministerial meeting on the upcoming regional vote, the Deputy Director argued for supporting strict regulation by citing the comparison between water treatment costs and industry transition costs - a framing that had not previously appeared in ministry communications.
When & Where
Inter-ministerial coordination meeting, 14 November 2025
Significance
This represents a shift from the ministry's previous neutral-to-cautious stance. The economic framing directly counters the Finance Ministry's concerns about industry impacts, potentially influencing the government's unified position ahead of the regional vote.
Contribution
Our policy brief on "The True Cost of Inaction" was shared with the Deputy Director's office three weeks prior. The specific figure cited in the meeting (treatment costs comparison) matches our briefing. A ministry contact confirmed the brief was discussed in preparatory meetings.
Significant contribution (corroborated)
Sources
Meeting notes (ministry contact) Internal communication log Policy brief delivery confirmation

Outcome 2: Conservative MP Withdraws Opposition Motion

Harvested: January 2026
Who Changed
Member of Parliament from the centre-right party, Environment Committee member
What Changed
The MP withdrew a draft parliamentary motion that would have called on the government to seek extended exemptions and longer transition periods in the regional restriction process. The motion had been scheduled for committee discussion.
When & Where
Parliamentary Environment Committee, 8 January 2026
Significance
This prevented a potential political signal that could have provided cover for the government to soften its position. The withdrawal also suggests the MP concluded there was insufficient support within their own party - indicating the "essential uses" messaging may be reducing conservative opposition more broadly.
Contribution
We cannot claim direct contribution to the MP's decision. However, our coalition's media campaign emphasising that "essential uses remain protected" received significant coverage in the weeks prior. The MP's withdrawal statement referenced "recognising that medical and safety uses are already exempted" - language consistent with our messaging.
Plausible contribution (indirect)
Sources
Committee agenda (before/after) MP's public statement AI-flagged: news coverage AI-flagged: social media

Linking Outcomes to Your Theory of Change

Harvested outcomes should be systematically connected back to your Theory of Change. This creates a learning loop: outcomes either provide evidence that your causal assumptions are holding, or they reveal where your theory needs revision.

Questions to Ask for Each Harvested Outcome

  • Which result in our Theory of Change does this outcome relate to?
  • Does this outcome support or challenge our causal assumptions?
  • What does this tell us about our strategy's effectiveness?
  • Should we adjust our activities based on what we've learned?
  • Are there unexpected outcomes that suggest new pathways to impact?

In the examples above, both outcomes relate to the same intermediate result but provide different types of evidence. Outcome 1 shows direct uptake of our messaging by a key actor. Outcome 2 shows a "defensive" win - blocking a negative development - with more indirect contribution. Together, they build a picture of how influence is accumulating toward the desired policy outcome.

Theory of Change: Defending Strong PFAS Regulation

This Theory of Change illustrates a civil society strategy for defending the scope and strength of an ongoing regional PFAS restriction process against industry efforts to weaken it, and preventing national-level political actors from undermining implementation.

Policy Context: The Regulation is Already Happening

A comprehensive PFAS restriction is already under development at the regional level. Regulatory bodies are evaluating the proposal, with a final decision expected within 1-2 years. The key question is not whether regulation will happen, but how strong it will be:

  • Industry lobbying is pushing to expand "essential use" exemptions and extend transition periods
  • Conservative/right-wing politicians may attempt national-level actions that undermine regional regulation
  • Member state votes will determine the final shape of the restriction

Strategy Focus: Defend Regulation Scope & Prevent National Undermining

Core approach: (1) Influence the national government's position to support strong regulation with limited exemptions in the upcoming regional vote; (2) Prevent conservative/right-wing politicians from taking national actions that jeopardize regional efforts; (3) Counter industry framing through sustained communication.

Key Causal Assumptions

Link Assumption Evidence / Risk
Advocacy → Government Position Officials will support strong regulation if: (1) fiscal case is compelling (€18B treatment costs > transition costs), (2) fear of job losses is addressed (essential uses protected), (3) they have independent information to counter industry claims, and (4) health impacts are made personally salient Medium: Strong enablers exist (public support, economic case, existing process) but barriers are significant (industry lobbying access, fear of job losses, pro-business culture)
Monitoring → Blocking Undermining Early detection of national-level weakening initiatives allows rapid response before they gain momentum; moderate conservatives can be persuaded not to support extreme positions Medium-High: Requires vigilance and quick mobilisation capacity; some politicians may be unreachable
Communication → Discourse Sustained counter-messaging can shift public discourse from industry's "blanket ban" frame to "protecting health from non-essential uses" Medium: Industry has significant resources; requires consistent, coordinated effort over time
Government Position → Regional Outcome This member state's vote and advocacy within regional bodies influences the final scope of the restriction (exemptions, transition periods) High: Large member states have significant influence; vote margins matter
No Undermining → Strong Implementation Blocking national weakening efforts ensures the regional regulation is implemented without delays, opt-outs, or enforcement gaps High: National politics directly affect implementation quality
Regulation → Threat Reduction Legal restrictions on PFAS in non-essential applications will lead to actual reductions in production, use, and discharge High: Regulatory restrictions have proven effective for similar chemicals; industry compliance is enforceable
Threat Reduction → Biophysical Improvement Reducing PFAS inputs will lead to gradual improvements in water and soil quality Medium: PFAS are highly persistent; improvements will be slow (decades) but preventing new contamination is essential

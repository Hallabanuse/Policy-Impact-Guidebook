Theory of Change - Generic Template

A Theory of Change (or Results Chain) shows the causal assumptions about how your actions will achieve intermediate results and longer-term goals. It transforms the "current state" (situation model) into a "desired state" by mapping the pathway from actions to outcomes.

From Situation Model to Results Chain

Your situation model shows the current state of the world - the threats, contributing factors, and their impacts on focal values. The Theory of Change flips this perspective: instead of showing what's causing harm, it shows what needs to change and how your actions will bring about that change. Each factor in your situation model can be converted to a desired result in your results chain.

Element Descriptions

Strategy / Action

A strategy is a broad approach or set of actions designed to achieve specific results. It represents what your team will do to address threats and achieve conservation outcomes.

Strategies should be:

  • Linked to your situation analysis (addressing identified threats or contributing factors)
  • Within your team's capacity to implement
  • Based on evidence or sound reasoning about what works
Activities are the specific tasks that implement a strategy. They help clarify how you expect to achieve specific results.

Intermediate Result

Intermediate results are the changes that occur between your actions and the ultimate conservation outcome. They typically involve changes in:

  • Behaviour: Actors change what they do (e.g., farmers adopt new practices)
  • Policy: Rules, regulations, or decisions change (e.g., law enacted)
  • Knowledge/Attitudes: People understand or believe differently (e.g., awareness increases)
  • Conditions: Circumstances change that enable other results (e.g., funding secured)
Include psycho-social states (beliefs, attitudes, knowledge) to clarify why activities lead to behavioural outcomes.

Threat Reduction Result

A threat reduction result shows that a direct threat has been reduced or eliminated. This is typically where your intermediate results translate into reduced pressure on biodiversity.

Examples:

  • Illegal logging reduced by X%
  • Pollution discharge decreased
  • Invasive species controlled
It is good practice to have an objective for the direct threat, unless it is outside your manageable interest.

Biophysical Result

Biophysical results show improvements in ecosystem conditions that result from threat reduction. These are the ecological changes that ultimately benefit your focal values.

Examples:

  • Water quality improved
  • Habitat connectivity restored
  • Native vegetation recovering

Focal Values

Biodiversity Focal Values are the species, ecosystems, or ecological processes you aim to conserve. They represent the ultimate conservation outcomes.

Human Wellbeing Focal Values show how conservation contributes to human outcomes - either through ecosystem services or directly through conservation actions.

Some projects may only intend to achieve threat reduction or intermediate results within their timeframe. The ultimate impact on focal values may be more implicit.

Objectives & Goals

Objectives are formal statements of outcomes (intermediate results) you believe are necessary to attain your goals. They specify the degree of change needed in the short and medium term.

Goals are linked to your focal values and represent the longer-term conservation outcomes you seek.

Good objectives and goals are SMART:

  • Specific - Clear about actors and behaviours
  • Measurable - Can be quantified or assessed
  • Achievable - Realistic given resources and context
  • Results-oriented - Focused on outcomes, not activities
  • Time-limited - Has a deadline or timeframe

Key Principles for Building Results Chains

1. Build on your Situation Model

Use your situation analysis as the basis. Each threat or contributing factor can become a result showing the desired change.

2. Make assumptions explicit

Your theory of change shows what you assume will happen. Use dotted lines to indicate where you're uncertain about causal links.

3. Include key actors

Results should specify who needs to change their behaviour and what change is needed. Be clear about the actors involved.

4. Define manageable interest

Be clear about which results are within your control vs. influence vs. interest. Not all projects aim for the final impact.
